Nimbat Taxonomy
Nimbats (scientific name Pterofelis Dodrili), are small, winged, felid-like creatures. They can grow to be anywhere between one (1) to four (4) feet tall, from base of the foot to tip of the ear. Their wings are composed of a membranous tissue, similar to bats (genus Chiroptera). They tend to be proportionate to their bodies by other bipedal species' standards. They are born with a total of seven (7) appendages- Two (2) arms, two (2) legs, two (2) wings, and a tail. There are four (4) to five (5) fingers on each hand, and three (3) to four (4) toes on each foot. Paw pads akin to cats exist on the bottoms of their toes, and the tips of their fingers. Their ears are tall and wide. Nimbats tend to live for a very long time; while they develop similar to humans when it comes to maturation cycles, their actual lifespan has an unknowable length.
"I’ve personally met a number of self-proclaimed millennia-old Nimbats, who don’t seem to be much physically older than those who have been alive for no more than 20 years. I’m unsure if I’ll ever know exactly how they work, as Nimbats seem to “dissolve” once they pass away, making dissection practically impossible."
Their eyes can be generalized into three distinct “types”. One of which is pupil and iris-less, and a convex “sparkly” iridescent cornea instead fills the eye. It tends to consist of a gradient of color, from anywhere from blue, to green, to red. Another has a distinctive separate iris and cornea, the iris tending to be a shade of the cornea. The third and final is what would be considered a “typical” eye, with cornea, iris, and pupil all separate. No matter the eye type, no nimbats are defaultly blind. They seem to be completely aware of all visual stimuli, and are able to navigate well.
Additionally, Nimbats who happen to lose parts of their bodies report that their lost parts sparkle and dissolve into the air, and the body that is left behind begins to heal immediately, quickly sealing the wound and stopping blood from escaping. However, they are unable to regrow limbs, and often turn to magic to augment themselves if they find themselves in that situation.
Nimbats are creatures with an inherent arcane or magical ability. A vast majority have been observed to display these abilities on a regular basis. More information can be found here.

Nimbat Subspecies
Below is a working outline of six observed Nimbat subspecies, while we conduct future studies and interviews over the physiology of these variants. It has been made noteworthy that like any typical case example of Pterofelis Dodrilis, they practice communicable sentience and are capable of expression, moral compasses, and advanced social structures. These subspecies are capable of interbreeding, and mixed offspring do not portray sterility, giving credence to the notion that “mixed” Nimbats are fairly common.
“You know Soteria, right? One of the Sect leaders in Protection? I know for a dang fact that one of his parents was a, uh, “cavernous” type. You ever see his eyes? How about the fact that he hides himself in a cloak more often than not? I’d ask him myself if the Sun bothers him a little, but, I can only get so close before I stammer and sweat like I’m in speech therapy.” - Anonymous

Woodland Nimbat

Examination of Scribing records and potential evolution timelines have created proposals within research groups that the Woodland subspecies might be the starting progenitor for the other examples on this page. The working theory suggests that early Woodland Nimbats migrated into different climate zones and biomes, for reasons we have yet to determine, and over millions of years had developed into these standards we see today. The Woodland Nimbat is often described as the “intrinsic balance” of physiology, without any exceptional traits that aid in their survival to the temperate forest climate. Most Colony Nimbats tend to be of the Woodland variety.
“Look at all the other types of Nimbats out there. You got the folks with freakishly big wings that cruise through storms, there’s the stocky fellas that survive the tundra, and there’s those really tall and athletic guys from the savannah. You know what we got? We got ORANGE.” - Brianna, Progression Elder

Tundra Nimbat

The Tundra Nimbat might have emerged not unlike how early humans could have crossed a massive bridge of ice to settle into the lands found in many latitudes above 60° N. Colder and windier environs have been known to induce some kind of mania within other subspecies (the most prominent symptom of which being incomprehensible vocalizations or ”speaking gibberish”). The Tundra Nimbat avoids this condition, largely due to their ability to trap more heat within their much thicker, poofier hides and more-pronounced body fat. This creates some issues with flight, since there is more weight to carry, and opening the wings is ill-advised for one trying to sustain warmth. Should flight be necessary, Tundra Nimbats often do so in very short bursts, akin to ducks or chickens, although they may let the wind try to do most of the work. Otherwise, this variant is largely grounded, opting to use their winged limbs to hold something tightly to their bodies.
“Don’t you ever think they’re out of shape just because they’re…poofy. Those snow-blown Nimbats carry luggage on their backs that probably weigh more than my house, and still have enough room to hold a kit or two in there.” - Anonymous

Grassland Nimbat

The first preliminary studies on the Grassland Nimbat suggested that a link between this subspecies, servals, and savannah cats had been formed at some point in evolutionary history. While such postulations have yet to be examined further, the resemblances are not unfounded. This variant is known for residing in areas with tall grasses and scarce tree growth. Taller ears and slender, lankier bodies make up their forms, and spots/rosettes adorn their fur. Their stature allows them to duck low to the ground to avoid being seen or perk up very high to gauge their surroundings. Grassland Nimbats are naturally athletic and nimble, able to break out into a sprint at speeds of 20mph (approx. 32kph). Their wings are wide and strong, but their membranes and bone structures do not encourage rapid beating. Instead, they are more prone to glide and move about the air constantly like hawks.
“I see this guy every Wednesday, he’s almost twice my height. But he’s so easily excited and keeps forgetting that he just blankets and smothers over me whenever he hugs me. It’s pretty funny when he has to duck to go through doors, heh.” - Jodan, 3rd-Shift Agriculture Pest Technician

Galerider Nimbat

Hailing from a tradition of nomadic lifestyles, the Galeriders are known to spend days to weeks in the sky, soaring at altitudes scoring up to 35,000 feet (approx. 10,668 meters). They roost in the higher reaches of mountainous regions, where they stop to feed themselves or attend to their young. To achieve this habitual living, their most striking feature—being their wings—tend to be much larger than even the most exceptional Woodland Nimbat, boasting of a whopping 2:1 ratio of wingspan to height. Since they spend most of their time traveling, magical practices tend to stay limited to something more immediate and utilitarian. From simple flames to wind manipulation, but never stepping into teleportation or portaling tricks.
“Record-holders in Scribing insist that the longest time in-flight lasted for 24 days, 5 hours, and 14 minutes—and this achievement was, naturally, produced by a Galerider. However, Nimbat denizens hailing from the mountaintops within [REDACTED] speak at length about a traditional legend depicting a Galerider having flown for an entire season (potentially four months) in order to herald the coming of summer’s wrath.”* - Researcher ???

Cave Nimbat

A primarily subterranean subspecies, Cave Nimbats are regarded as the oddest of the bunch. They are described as “scrunkly”, and alien. They have much larger eyes, but are pupilless. Their paws are thinner, with sharper claws, used to climb rock more easily. Their wings are strong, but are not meant for gliding, hovering, or flying- but for jumping, used as a way to “boost” to higher cliff faces within caves. They have shorter to very little fur, and large ears, leading them to be often more soft spoken as a result. Their vision dips deeply into the infrared spectrum, and it is reported they have a sensitivity to UV light, including sunlight. This does not mean they are nocturnal, instead opting to wear hoods to protect their eyes from direct sunlight if they are to venture out of their cave during daylight hours.
“Soteria’s told me that they use infrared lighting in their caves, and are able to see perfectly well. Note to self: Ask Acquisition if they’re able to get me a pair of night vision goggles…”

Aquatic Nimbat

These water-dwelling Nimbats bear a physiology that closely resembles mustelids, however they also bear a few features that are decidedly aquatic—thus the colloquial nomenclature of “Aquatic Nimbats” was discerned. This type is known to live on islands, especially within archipelagos and similar geographical arrangements. In the water, they are excellent hunters, using their wings, tail, and webbed digits to quickly close the distance to catch fish, crustaceans, and even small turtles. Prominent gill-like markings can be seen on their shoulders, which can attract parasites to be trapped and eaten as well. Due to their heavy wings being more suited for mobility in water, they are flightless. When they are not hunting or traversing, they stay in areas closer to the water, such as in damp, rocky areas that are kept moist by the tide.
“First recorded encounter with mustelid-based subvariant, June 13th. Personal forays on the coast of [REDACTED] with the intended purpose of documenting sea turtle behavior (and because I just like looking at them) resulted in a happenstance and mutual meeting. The subsequent personality was quick to engage in investigative yet intrusively affectionate behavior (my face STILL tickles) before extending an invitation to partake in the tide with them. I…decided to brave my aversion to water, in order to get more answers out of them. After publishing this account, I’m going to take two days of personal time to decompress after the intense cross-behavioral exchange.” -Katell, Preservation Elder
